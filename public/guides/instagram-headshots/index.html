What Makes a Good Instagram Headshot

Three things separate a profile photo people trust from one they scroll past.

The crop. Your face should fill most of the frame — shoulders up, or just head and neck. Instagram's circular crop cuts off anything near the edges, so a photo that looks fine as a square can lose your forehead or chin in the app.

Eye line. Eyes roughly a third of the way down the frame, looking at or near the camera. Off-angle glances read as candid on a regular photo; on a tiny profile circle, they just read as unfocused.

Background contrast. A background that's a different tone than your skin and clothes keeps you from blending into the frame. Solid, slightly out-of-focus backgrounds beat busy ones almost every time — more on that below.

Instagram Photo Size & Crop Specs

Instagram displays profile pictures at 110x110 pixels on desktop, though it renders larger on mobile and in the story-ring view. The platform accepts larger uploads and compresses down, so upload higher resolution than the display size — at least 320x320, ideally 500x500 or more, so it stays sharp on high-DPI phone screens.

Because the final image displays in a circle, keep your subject centered with a margin around the edges. Anything you don't want cropped — logos on a shirt, text, the top of your head — should sit at least 10-15% inside the square's border. Upload a square image (1:1 ratio) rather than trusting Instagram's auto-crop to make good decisions on a rectangular photo.

Instagram Headshots by Use Case

Business or creator profile. Neutral or branded-color background, direct eye contact, minimal expression extremes — a slight smile reads as approachable without looking staged. This is the photo doing the most work; it should look consistent with your bio and content, not like a different person.

Personal branding. More room for personality — genuine smile, casual clothing, a background that hints at what you do (a bookshelf, a workspace, an outdoor setting). The goal shifts from "trustworthy stranger" to "recognizable person."

Team or company grid consistency. If you're setting photos for a whole team, consistency beats individual flair. Same background color or location, same crop distance, same lighting setup. A grid of ten different backgrounds and angles looks like nobody agreed on a plan — because nobody did.

Common Mistakes

  • Cropped foreheads or chins. Test your photo in an actual circle crop before uploading — what looks fine square often loses the top of the head.
  • Backlighting. A bright window behind you turns your face into a silhouette. Face the light source instead.
  • Busy backgrounds. Bookshelves, crowds, patterned walls compete with your face for attention in a tiny frame.
  • Old photos. A five-year-old headshot creates a mismatch the moment someone meets you or sees your Stories.
  • Group photo crops. Cropping yourself out of a group shot usually leaves a stray arm or half a stranger's face in frame.

FAQ

What size should an Instagram profile headshot be?
Instagram displays profile pictures at 110x110 pixels but upload a square image at least 320x320, ideally 500x500 or larger, so it stays sharp on high-resolution screens.

Should I smile in an Instagram headshot?
For business or creator profiles, a slight, closed or relaxed smile reads as approachable without looking overly posed. For personal accounts, a genuine smile usually outperforms a neutral expression.

Can I use an AI headshot for Instagram?
Yes — Instagram doesn't require a verified real photo. AI-generated headshots work fine for a profile picture. They're not appropriate for anything requiring an officially verified photo, like a passport or government ID.

How much do professional headshots cost?
Costs vary widely by city, photographer experience, and session length — most sessions land between $150 and $450, with a U.S. median around $250. See our cost comparison guide for a fuller breakdown by scenario.

What's the best background for an Instagram headshot?
A solid or softly blurred background that contrasts with your skin tone and clothing. Avoid patterns, clutter, or anything bright enough to pull focus from your face.
